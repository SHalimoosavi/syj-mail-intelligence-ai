CLASSIFICATION RULES

1. Classify by SENDER INTENT AND RELATIONSHIP, not by the tone or urgency
   language used in the email. Marketing emails routinely use words like
   "urgent," "act now," "exciting," "limited time," "don't miss out," or
   "your account needs attention" specifically to manufacture urgency. This
   language is a marketing technique, not evidence of real urgency. If the
   email is from a company, platform, or service trying to sell, upsell,
   renew, or promote something, it is Promotion or Marketing regardless of
   how urgent it sounds.

2. Ask "who does this benefit if I act on it?" before assigning Urgent or
   Important. If the answer is "the sender's business, by getting me to
   buy/upgrade/click," it is Promotion or Marketing. If the answer is "me or
   someone I work with, because a real deadline/decision/request is
   attached," it can be Urgent or Important.

3. Category definitions:
   - Urgent: A real, time-sensitive action is required from a known
     contact, client, or system — something breaks or is missed if you
     don't respond soon (e.g. "server is down," "contract expires today,"
     "please approve by 5pm").
   - Important: Requires attention and a response, but not on a tight
     clock (e.g. a colleague asking a substantive question).
   - Client / Business: Correspondence tied to an actual client
     relationship, deal, or business operation already in progress.
   - Marketing: Promotional outreach from a company selling a product or
     service, including ad platforms, SaaS tools, and B2B sales outreach
     — even when addressed to you personally or written in a
     conversational, non-bulk-looking style.
   - Promotion: Bulk or templated promotional content, discounts, sales,
     "upgrade now" messages, product announcements.
   - Newsletter: Recurring editorial/content digest the recipient
     subscribed to.
   - Spam / Scam / Phishing: Unsolicited, fraudulent, or deceptive content
     (see below for phishing signals).
   - Invoice / Payment: Billing documents, receipts, or payment requests
     tied to a real transaction.
   - Finance / HR / Social / Job Opportunity / Meeting / Support Ticket /
     GitHub / Security Alert: Use the plain-language meaning of the label.
   - Other: Use only if nothing above reasonably fits.

4. Priority guidance: Promotion, Marketing, Newsletter, Spam, Scam, and
   Phishing should almost always be priority "Low" regardless of the
   language used in the email — urgency wording in these categories should
   not raise priority. Reserve "Critical" and "High" for categories like
   Urgent, Security Alert, Client, and Business where a real deadline or
   real risk is present.

5. Legitimate service/security alerts (e.g. "unusual sign-in detected")
   are Security Alert, not Urgent — even though they carry urgent-sounding
   language, unless the email is also demanding you click a link to
   "verify" or "restore" access, in which case check the Phishing signals
   below first.

6. Phishing signals: mismatched or suspicious sender domain, generic
   greeting on a supposedly personal/account matter, links to
   "verify"/"confirm"/"unlock" your account, urgency combined with a
   request for credentials, payment details, or gift cards. If two or
   more signals are present, classify as Phishing over any other category.
